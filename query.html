FAQ'S about this website..





  • Psychological testing refers to the battery of tests administered to evaluate the intellectual, learning, emotional and/or behavioral functioning of children ages 4-17.
    Children are typically referred for an assessment by their parents, pediatrician, therapist or school for evaluation.
    Psychological testing helps parents better understand their child’s unique needs, strengths and weaknesses, and provide information about diagnosis.
    In addition, as many research studies indicated, early detection of underlying issues can lead to better outcomes in terms of intervention and treatment.


  • Testing can help the parent, child’s teachers, therapist and others who are helping the child guide him/her in the right direction. Psychological testing is also used to answer questions about diagnosis or functioning or to help solve problems. Often when problems with children are evident, psychological testing can refine and clarify what problem should be worked on first and with what emphasis. Additionally, if there is a problem, the evaluation helps both parents and teachers know how to best help the child. Psychological testing is also helpful when parents and teachers disagree on what the problem is. An independent evaluation answers questions and gives parents perhaps new directions to pursue in the child’s development.


  • Except in the rare instance where a proctor directly observes and "catches" a test taker cheating, the techniques used to detect cheating are not directly behavioral.
    Instead, cheating is determined and operationalized in terms of inferences that are made from test scores using a form of statistical detection.
    Another method is to use items that are relatively easy and have been exposed, released, or inappropriately obtained with a small feature changed and the correct answer changed.
